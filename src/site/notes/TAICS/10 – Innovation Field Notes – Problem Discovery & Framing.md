# Key learnings
This week, we've looked into the art of problem formulation. I say art because it's not as simple as it may seem. A strong problem formulation is key in the innovation process. It's a way to clarify the complexity in which we work, and provides us with a starting point to test and validate our assumptions. A strong problem formulation is actionable.

What really stuck with me is the understanding that discovering problems is systematic. We need to dissect the problem, dissect our assumptions of it, dissect the context in which it occurs, and cut through our hallucinations in every step of that process. The question "Why?" is central here, we need to go deep. When we believe we've found the answer, ask why again. 

We were introduced to the Problem Discovery Canvas which is a tool to prototype our problem formulation, similar to the Business Model Canvas used to prototype a business. By doing this, we create an actionable *hypothesis* to work with.

# Personal reflections
I resonate a lot with this part of the innovation process. I'm strong when it comes to asking questions and digging beyond the surface. I haven't thought as explicitly about problems being *repeated patterns* and *situated* before. But of course, urgent problems occur frequently during specific conditions. 

I believe the capability to discover and frame problems is one of the most relevant skills looking forward. The world is both getting more complex and difficult to trust. I see a clear pattern in people getting more and more lost in what to believe and how to navigate the world. Different information sources are contradicting each other, and AI is going from not only generating misleading and realistic-looking content but also interacting with people online, often without them even realizing that it's a bot. These issues are only going to grow, which increases the demand for clarity and truth. We need to maintain our grip of how reality *actually is* when our perception of it is increasingly distorted. 

# Questions & curiosities
It's a struggle to know when to stop asking why and when we've truly found the core problem. We need an anchor here, something to rely on, but what could that be? There's a tension between the strive for a final, definite answer, and knowing that the context is constantly changing and always including hidden and unpredictable aspects. There's a balance needed here, and again, perhaps we shouldn't diminish our gut feeling in navigating it.

There's also the tension in needing objectivity, but as a human only being able to experience the world subjectively. No matter how hard we try to, we cannot be 100% objective. 

# Future actions
Strong problem formulation is one of the key capabilities I want to focus on as it lays the foundation for the whole project. I will continue using the Problem Discovery Canvas as a prototyping tool as it was a good starting point for creating something tangible. I will use opportunities to practise acting on my problem formulation and iterating it along the way as I can see this helping me avoid getting lost in abstraction and more grounded in action. 

# Methods & tools
**Problem Discovery Canvas:** a good tool for prototyping a problem formulation and a starting point to write down our initial assumptions about a situation. It can directly inform possible next steps in the innovation process like creating a stakeholder map. 

It's helpful because it has specific questions to start thinking about. It has constraints, which we've learned can actually make it easier to progress than roaming freely. But of course, constraints can also be limiting. As we know, no solution fits all, and this might also be true for discovering problems. Perhaps not all problems are going to be best approached by these specific questions.

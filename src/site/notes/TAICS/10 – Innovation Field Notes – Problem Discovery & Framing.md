# Key learnings
This week, we've looked into the art of problem discovery and formulation. I say art because it's not as simple as it may seem. A strong problem formulation is key in the innovation process. It's a way to clarify the complexity in which we work, and provides us with a starting point to test and validate our assumptions. A strong problem formulation is actionable.

What really stuck with me is the understanding that discovering problems is systematic. We need to dissect the problem, dissect our assumptions of it, dissect the context in which it occurs, and cut through our hallucinations in every step of that process. The question "Why?" is central here, we need to go deep. When we believe we've found the answer, ask why again. 

We were introduced to the 5WH1 framework and Problem Discovery Canvas which are tools to uncover and prototype our problem formulation, similar to the Business Model Canvas used to prototype a business. By doing this, we create an actionable *hypothesis* to work with. We then dug deeper into the context of the problem space with PESTEL analysis, stakeholder mapping/matrix and SMART goals that ultimately informed our project brief.

# Personal reflections
I resonate a lot with this part of the innovation process. I'm strong when it comes to asking questions and digging beyond the surface. I haven't thought as explicitly about problems being *repeated patterns* and *situated* before. But of course, urgent problems occur frequently during specific conditions. 

I believe the capability to discover and frame problems is one of the most relevant skills looking forward. The world is both getting more complex and difficult to trust. I see a clear pattern in people getting more and more lost in what to believe and how to navigate the world. Different information sources are contradicting each other, and AI is going from not only generating misleading and realistic-looking content but also interacting with people online, often without them even realizing that it's a bot. These issues are only going to grow, which increases the demand for clarity and truth. We need to maintain our grip of how reality *actually is* when our perception of it is increasingly distorted. This is where systematic tools like PESTEL and stakeholder mapping can provide a lot of value. 

# Questions & curiosities
It's a struggle to know when to stop asking why and when we've truly found the core problem. We need an anchor here, something to rely on, but what could that be? There's a tension between the strive for a final, definite answer, and knowing that the context is constantly changing and always including hidden and unpredictable aspects. There's a balance needed here, and again, perhaps we shouldn't diminish our gut feeling in navigating it.

There's also the tension in needing objectivity, but as a human only being able to experience the world subjectively. No matter how hard we try to, we cannot be 100% objective. We will always have assumptions, previous experiences, emotional reactions and other aspects affecting our judgement. What's important is to be aware of this.

# Future actions
Strong problem formulation is one of the key capabilities I want to focus on as it lays the foundation for the whole project. I will continue using the Problem Discovery Canvas as a prototyping tool as it was a good starting point for creating something tangible. I will use opportunities to practise acting on my problem formulation and iterating it along the way as I can see this helping me avoid getting lost in abstraction and more grounded in action. 

I will definitely continue using the stakeholder mapping as I found this very helpful for identifying possible touchpoints for value creation. I realized that it can indicate whether a new product is needed, or if existing stakeholders can actually already provide what others need and that the problem is rather the missing touchpoint between them. So, it may inform us not only about what role different stakeholders have for us, but also what type of solution may be more fitting (a product, service, process, something else). 

# Methods & tools
**5W1H Framework/Problem Framing Canvas:** these are good tools for prototyping a problem formulation and a starting point to write down our initial assumptions about a situation. The 5W1H can be valuable to discover a problem, whilst the Problem Framing Canvas is good for diving a bit deeper into the problem. 

These tools can directly inform possible next steps in the innovation process like creating a stakeholder map.They're helpful because they have specific questions to start thinking about. They have constraints, which we've learned can actually make it easier to progress than roaming freely. But of course, constraints can also be limiting. As we know, no solution fits all, and this might also be true for discovering problems. Perhaps not all problems are going to be best approached by these specific questions. 

**PESTEL analysis**: this is a tool to delve into the context in which our problem exists. It can help us understand underlying aspects that may affect our problem – Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental  – and how these dimensions may relate to each other. Together, this can reveal a much more comprehensive understanding of the environment surrounding the problem. 

**Stakeholder mapping/matrix**: mapping out stakeholders is good when wanting to understand who is affected by our problem and how. This lets us understand what interests, needs, influence and risks that are associated with different stakeholders, and can enable us to rank their importance and position in our project.

All of these tools help us clarify underlying relationships and impact within our problem space. Using them systematically lays a good foundation for defining SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, Timed). The SMART goals then provide a clear and tangible plan going forward. However, there's still a risk of missing crucial pieces of information that affects everything in our following decisions. We also need to be aware of the fact that, even though our process is structured when working with these tools, we are still working with *assumptions* that need to be continuously validated.

**Livet efter Framtiden – Torbjörn Elensky** (book)
This book addresses the complex political landscape of our time. It serves as a guide to navigate our current time in which answers are constantly demanded, yet simple solutions are increasingly difficult to provide. This is relevant to me because we've seen that most issues we tackle exist within a political landscape. I believe deepening my knowledge about this strengthens my systems thinking, future-oriented perspective and may increase the accuracy of my predictions. 

**Making Sense – Sam Harris** (book)
A collection from Sam's podcast on intellectual conversations together with for example Nick Bostrom, Max Tegmark, Daniel Kahneman, Timothy Snyder and Anil Seth. Some of these names have had a big influence on my own thinking within topics like AI, ethics, physics and philosophy. This book allows clearer thinking around the complex issues in our time. It's well aligned with responsible action-taking towards a sustainable future where organic life merges with the digital.

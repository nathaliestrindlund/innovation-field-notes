# W13 – Innovation Field Notes – Creativity & Ideation
## Key learnings
Ideas can't be directly translated from one brain to another. They are abstract and fleeting. And even though we can see them clearly in our mind, it's practically impossible to make someone else see that exact image. So we need to use ways around that to communicate our ideas. One example of that is through metaphors. They can be very valuable for making people think about our ideas in certain ways. Through metaphors, we can nudge others into the means or feeling we want our idea to embody. 

Another way is through associations. It's like charades or the game where one person gets a word and has to use synonyms to make the other one understand what the word is. The challenge is to make someone understand what we mean without showing them the exact thing. 

In previous courses, we've discussed whether something can truly be new. And I still don't know the answer to this question but what I know now is that everything can still be (infinitely?) adapted, modified, combined, and ultimately evolved. And as I discuss in the next section, these activities might be invaluable when looking at how our world is developing right now and what needs are emerging from that.

## Personal reflections
As we've ideated a lot about inclusive solutions this week, I've thought deeply about the concept of inclusivity. Inclusivity isn't just important because we should comply with laws and consider ethical frameworks. I believe that inclusivity points us to something that goes way beyond such superficialities, and that is highly relevant to us in this particular time.

For centuries, we have designed for optimization. We strive for everything to become bigger, better, faster, more powerful than before. In a way, optimization can be likened to reduction. We identify aspects of products and components that produce the most bang for buck, and we put all our effort into developing those things while excluding the rest. We strip things down to their core and remove all excess. And as we know, everything we do comes at a cost of something else. I saw a video that resonated strongly with me in saying that optimizing comes at the cost of beauty. Compare modern architecture to that from 300 years ago. Think about the amount of mental illness and burnout related to our constant strive to get better at what we do. And still, we know that we can never become perfect. Optimizing just isn't natural, because life can't be reduced like that. It is complex and chaotic. Similarly, I don't think humans are meant to live in a world where all "excess" is stripped away. 

Go out in nature and look around you. Anywhere you look, nature is full of color, of movement, of textures, of sounds. Everywhere you look – life is rich. We have always told stories about this richness, even in our explanations of how the world was created. If you believe in a God, these stories paint vivid images of light bursting through darkness, waters crashing and colliding, matter forming through motion, tension and release. If you believe in science, the story is different but the intensity is the same. Life began with a bang, exploding energy forming sparkling star dust in every color, extreme temperatures, colliding particles and unfathomable speeds. No matter what you believe about its origin, the nature of life is everything but simple and stripped down. It emerged through chaos and it's abundant, spectacular, brilliant, vibrant, miraculous. So why do we design to resist our nature? 

In recent years, there's been a clear countermovement to this. Trends in design, fashion and other creative industries include lifestyles and aesthetic themes of the organic, the "real" and being outdoors. The 2026 trend report from Ungdomsbarometern shows that the hottest themes amongst Generation Alpha include "granny hobbies", blue collar work, living mindfully, maximalism & the unpolished. Interestingly, they show a bigger interest in religion compared to older generations.

I think this tells us something about us longing to come back to our roots. We are meant for radiance, richness, vibrant experiences and deep connections to feel like we belong to this world and everything within it. In our pursuit to optimize, we seem to have forgotten that, not only does perfection not exist, but nobody *wants* perfection in the first place. We want the raw, the authentic, the real, the organic – because that's our nature. In my opinion, beauty is a greatly misunderstood and underrated dimension of life that gets constantly overlooked – which comes with consequences on our existential wellbeing that also seem to go unnoticed. We design and live in a world that forces us to be ignorant to beauty, and thereby also enjoying life fully. Especially in the West. The Eastern philosophies are, in many ways, much better at preserving human nature on a spiritual and existential level – and recognizing our need for experiencing the richness of life. 

I believe we are at a significant point in time where we can actually do something about this. At the same time as the need for rediscovering our nature grows, we are developing very powerful tools that can actually replace our optimization labor – so that we can more fully focus on reconnecting with what brings us meaning and belonging. Rick Rubin says that humans are meant to create. And there might be some truth in that since we're at least not stopping doing it. So, maybe one way of coming closer back to our nature is by doing and creating things the same way we were created. We need more color, more emotion, more extravagance. We need to INCLUDE more, so that we can experience life fuller. This way, we may design things in ways that include the parts of us that were neglected, rejected and excluded as a result from a world that's hyperfixated on optimization.

## Questions & curiosities
I wonder why we choose to resist our nature. Of course, it's all about money and power in the end. It's easy to control people when stripping away their essence so that all that remains is more or less zombies. I think this is as interesting as it is horrible. I don't want to contribute to that, but it's also difficult to design good things for a world that doesn't favor them (at least not the ones in charge of it). This is one of my biggest pains, or concerns, or maybe even griefs. I have no solution to this, and I'm not sure there is one. Although I believe that AI is one of very few things that shows potential to change this. But it requires that the ones developing it and curating it knows and wants to do it, which I highly doubt.

## Future actions
My biggest takeaway from this week is that I will honor and utilize my creativity more. Because if I'm right in my text above, quality of life isn't just about convenience and ease, it's about experiencing things in a richer way. My creativity is what will enable me to design things in this way. I will think more about how I can allow for more richness into my solutions and how I can help bring people back to our nature. 

## Methods & tools
**Random Word Association (brainstorming)** and **Visual Confetti (brainstorming)**: 
These brainstorming methods are great for quickly coming up with a starting point for producing ideas. They force us to think about connections between one random word or picture and our problem at hand, which immediately prompts new thoughts. Especially the Random Word Association proved to work well for me to understand aspects of my problems through different metaphors which gave me a pair of new eyes. However, I think these methods might have given me more value if used in a more strategic way. Now, they generated a lot of irrelevant thoughts and very little was actually used in the end. 

**Negative Ideation (brainstorming)**: 
With this method, we think about the issues we know are included within our problem, and how we can make those even worse. Then they're reversed into their opposite. It was surprisingly simple to come up with bad ideas and they were often very creative. So, this method seemed to work well for getting the creativity flowing and I enjoyed it because we're rarely allowed to come up with bad ideas. It also made me more aware of aspects that I might have overlooked or taken for granted before. 

**The Six Thinking Hats method (evaluating):** 
This method allows us to approach our ideas from several different perspectives by taking on different "hats" that represent specific roles. They could be anything from an optimist, pessimist, end user and law maker. Here, we start getting into systems thinking and I think methods like these are very efficient for starting to think about how our ideas could be perceived from different angles. But it's very high-level and the insights might be vague and based on guessing, which is a limitation. 

**Business potential, User Value, Feasibility & Passion approach (evaluating)**: 
This model evaluates our idea based on its value for companies, users, real-world constraints and our own excitement about it. So, now we're evaluating an idea based on something tangible and how well it could actually work in reality. I especially appreciated that it encouraged us to think about how excited we actually are to work with our different ideas. And by grading them from 1-10 across these axes, we can compare them against each other and get a quick overview of which one might be most valuable to pursue. However, we're mainly working with assumptions at this stage and still need to validate to actually *know* which one would be the best.

**SCAMPER model (evaluating):** 
The SCAMPER model is a tool to refine ideas by thinking about how aspects of them can be subsidized, combined, adapted, modified, put to another use, eliminated or reversed. At this stage, we probably have an idea about what issues are related to our idea and our problem, and this tool was very valuable to iterate on our ideas by exploring new ways of approaching these issues. It was pretty clear that an idea is never "complete", they are very malleable. And it can be difficult to know what actually makes an idea better.

# W16 – Innovation Field Notes – The Business
## Key learnings
No matter how short the timeframe is, you can *always* adapt to deliver something at the end of it. This week was a clear evidence of it. Time constraints is a recurring reason for people to blame why they didn't do something, but I'm reiterating what I've written previously that *time prioritization* is one crucial factor that dictates how coherent the delivery ends up.

One question was central and reoccurring this whole week: who is the customer, and where are they? Speaking to the CEO of Minc gave us valuable insight into the product-market-value fit when saying "established companies tend to buy from suppliers within the category they’re looking for solutions" – reaching out specifically to you has to make sense for the customer depending on what they need. Positioning yourself unintuitively or framing yourself unclearly can be fatal for your product. 

## Personal reflections
This week, there was a high ambition and strong personalities within the team, and that comes with many challenges. We struggled to understand each other and communicate clearly, which puts a strain on us when time is very limited. Some members took initiative to make additional things that weren't agreed on beforehand, which made it very difficult to align everything. It's hard to keep it simple, especially when the team members want to do more than what's necessary (which honestly just makes the final delivery more confusing and incoherent). But in the end, we managed to deliver something.

> [!quote]
> "Work expands so as to fill the time available for its completion."
> [Parkinson's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parkinson%27s_law) 

This principle says that if you have, in this case, 72 hours to do the work, the work will take 72 hours to do. Thinking back, there is actually not one professional setting where I haven't completed the delivery in time – because **we naturally adapt.** And I think the time prioritization gets more important the less time you have. The less time we have, the more structured we need to be. We definitely weren't always prioritizing our time well this week, which led to a few gaps in our coherence and logic that ultimately weakened our credibility.

Something that surprised me was that no team really mentioned ethical or sustainability aspects when pitching their concepts (including us). These considerations are highly valued in the Nordic market and could probably drastically strengthen the credibility of your offer compared to others – especially in the gaming industry which has a lot of pressure in ethical dimensions like misrepresentation, feeding addiction, making money off of young people. 

On another note, there's something I've been reflecting on throughout the problem but became especially apparent this week, is the level at which AI is being discussed. I've spent several years researching AI on my own, and to me its clear that the impact of AI is unlike any and every technology we've seen. It feels surprising that in a program centered around digital innovation and responsible change, this discussion around AI remains at a very surface level.

As we had time to dig deeper into something of our own choice this week, I finally read the whole [AI 2027](https://ai-2027.com/). This is the most comprehensive projection I've come across about the upcoming years with AI, written by big names in the industry. To me, this is exactly the kind of contextual awareness we talk about as crucial, and quite frankly, need to have, but somehow we're neglecting the AI disruption almost entirely...? I don't see any industry or business area that won't be affected by AI directly, or indirectly. And this is not the future. It's happening at rapid and increasing speeds right now. What I find most frustrating is the gap between what's being adressed and the scale of the questions that AI actually raises. To me, the most important aspects arent about its usage, but its consequences: how it completely reshapes human relationships, communication, identity and social structures. We should ask questions like how it influences how we express ourselves, relate to meaning and work, or how it can be used to shape or amplify narratives at a large scale which can directly influence people's perception of reality. I expected to explore these questions more meaningfully in this problem, especially in relation to sustainability and responsibility. Like how[ AI can enable 79% of the Sustainability Development Goals](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-019-14108-y). 

Building a sustainable business, I believe, at this point in time means to understand the dynamics of AI and learn how to apply it to a particular domain. Because if we don't, we will quickly fall behind. Read the AI 2027 if you're still in distrust of that.

## Questions & curiosities
I'm very conflicted as to why considering ethical and sustainability aspects wasn't an explicit part of the brief, more than "create an economically viable business plan". What about social consequences? What about choice of production chains and partners? In a program about responsible change for the future, I think we should pay a lot more focus on these aspects. 

Secondly, why aren't we learning more about economy? Why are we expected to build Business Model Canvases and viable solutions without having any base understanding of economics? This is something highly relevant to innovators and entrepreneurs and something I know I need to understand better. At the least to feel more confident in the field. 

Finally, why aren't we learning more about AI on a deeper and meaningful level when talking about how to create a viable business? Read the last paragraphs of my Personal Reflection to see what I mean. 

## Future actions
I will work on KISS (keeping it simple, stupid). I have never been very good at this and am still not. But there's a difference now in at least understanding and valuing this perspective. I understand that the more factors we add and the more we try to cover, the more we also risk losing the coherence and clarity of our message. 

I will also work on my ability to communicate priorities better with my team mates, especially in time-pressured situations. We first need to be aligned internally to be able to communicate it coherently externally. This misaligned was quite obvious to me during our presentation, as it showed that our understanding of different parts of our project was actually different amongst the group members, even if I thought we had a shared understanding. I was wrong :)

## Methods & tools
**Business Canvas** (Excel sheet): this is a good tool to translate a concept into a financial reality by outlining all the incoming and outgoing costs. This proved to be very helpful to get a better understanding of, and being able to assess, whether our idea is actually sustainable over time. It can highlight specific points in our chain that would break in the current business model, and where we have wiggle room. It exposes our assumptions and gaps in our logic, and clearly shows how a small tweak can have a big effect on other aspects within the model. At the same time, this business canvas is highly uncertain and based on our best guess/estimate rather than validated data. We could have been way off in our estimate. So in early stages, we need to take it with a grain of salt, and iterate it over time as we gather real, validated data.

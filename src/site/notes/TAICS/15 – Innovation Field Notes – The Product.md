# W15 – Innovation Field Notes – The Product
## Key learnings
VALIDATE OR DIE. How? Again, talk to people. Even when time is a scarce resource, this is what we should priorotize. Because at this stage, what we need is numbers. No matter how well we can explain our concept and tell a story about its impact on people, we need to back it up by data for anyone to trust us that we've put in the work to validate that people actually want our idea (as we've talked a lot about previous weeks). In addition to this, we need to collect GOOD data – which means to collect it ourselves to ensure that it's tailored to our specific idea in our specific context.

We've done this by conducting a MVE: Minimum Viable Experiment, which was a systematic testing and evaluating of our most pressing assumptions through a quick-and-dirty prototype. 

## Personal reflections
This week really challenged my mindset. There conditions put pressure on us as a lot to be done in a very short amount of time. Just finding people that are willing to invite you into their home to test a prototype together is hard. To find elderly people with physical impairments who are available today afternoon and willing to participate in our experiment, and find enough of them to be able to extract patterns from the data collected, is tough. It's easy to blame the time constraints and say that it's impossible. Because it nearly is. But what if we turn it around and ask ourselves "so instead of focusing on what we cannot do, what CAN we do?" I think this is what made me get through this week in one piece. I stayed positive and solution-oriented. I was forced to prioritize where my efforts would pay off most, and by being strategic, it also felt more okay to not succeeding with everything – because I knew that I would at least get what's most important right now. 

I'm also happy that we chose to have a kind of roleplaying scenario/Wizard of Oz during our Experience Theatre. I really wanted our audience to feel for our vulnerable target group and understand the very many ways that digital authentication is an issue for physically impaired. It's a really complex problem, which affects people a lot. I believe that our storytelling format was really effective to communicate this. By creating 4 different scenarios representing 4 users with different variations of this issue (kind of like 4 different personas), and emboyding these personas through 4 actors, gave them a voice, a face, and a view about how their relationship with their relatives is intertwined into this problem as well. I think it would be very difficult to communicate this without strong storytelling. Empathy is a very powerful dimension we can tap into to engage people and make them feel what's difficult to express in words.

## Questions & curiosities
What do we do when we actually can't engage with real users, but have to present real numbers? This is still an issue that I'm not totally sure of. In my mind, the best we can do is simulate users, having people outside the target group roleplay as them, or simulate with general data with the help of AI. But there's of course a limit to when this is no longer appropriate. What do we do then?

## Methods & tools
**Wizard of Oz** (prototyping/testing method). This experiential simulation format can be used as a prototyping and user research method. We used it as a combination of both this week. It has a front stage (what the participant/user interacts with) and a backstage (a human operator who controls parts of the interaction make it feel real, when it's actually fake). The positive thing with this method is that it's a very cheap and effective way of testing and/or experiencing interactions with a concept. It makes use of our ability to imagine things, which is very powerful. It's dynamic and offers nuances as it allows us to adapt to changes in the environment and tweak aspects of the experience in real-time. That makes it very effective when both communicating ideas and exploring how different dimensions of our concept can be tweaked to achieve certain effects or results. However, it's tricky to simulate a scenario to "pass" for being real. The participants are aware of it being fake, which always risks producing skewed or biased results, and means that we need to treat this data carefully.

**Pretotyping** (prototyping method). A very [quick-and-dirty type of prototype](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3sUozPcH4fY), as described by Alberto Savoia. It's basically a prototype that can be built in minutes, or hours. I think this has its place in certain situations of the innovation process, like now, when having very little time to validate an idea to be able to proceed. It's quick and cheap – but that also requires that we're super careful about how we introduce it to people so that they don't focus on the fact that it's quick and cheap. It's actually very hard to create the right circumstances for people to focus on what we want them to. For example, I don't believe a pretotype would be good when testing the look and feel of a complex UI, or brand. Because the quick and dirty aspects are directly influencing their perception of its"social status", which might not reflect its actual positioning.

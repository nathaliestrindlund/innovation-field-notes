We've really seen that validation, alignment and coherence are iterative processes that help us move away from the understanding of innovation as linear but rather a continuous process that requires perseverance and grit.

## Questions & curiosities
I want to circle back to the uncertainties I wrote about in the beginning of the week as they are still key to me.

**I believe we need a clearer grasp of the status quo.**
Early on in the previous course, a change agent was defined as ‚Äúsomeone who changes the status quo and drives sustainable transformation that creates value and positive impact for people, organizations and society‚Äù. One thing I‚Äôm lacking here is a greater discussion on what this status quo actually looks like. So much is happening around us, just within the technological development alone, and we need to keep up with that if we want to be relevant in it. In our Emerging Tech course, we touched on how robotics and AI is used in military and war. This is a great example of an area that I've deliberately chosen to avoid looking into, because it's too overwhelming. But we NEED to look into these things ‚Äì¬†because this IS what's happening around us, right now. We need to be aware of that, and we need to discuss how to handle it to be able to avoid a collective feeling of paralysis. 

**That leads to the question... are we future-ready?**
Do we have the tools, frameworks, skills and knowledge to understand, navigate and responsibly transform the very complex problems we are facing? This isn‚Äôt just a question for this programme but in a broader sense too. How can we be sure that we‚Äôre well equipped when everything is unpredictable and constantly changing? How do we handle resistance to change when it is constantly happening? And most importantly, are we asking the hard questions and facing the uncomfortable truths needed to ensure a viable way forward?

This also relates to what I brought up during the seminar about innovation not always aligning with business interests. Most business acknowledge that innovation is good and needed ‚Äì but aren't necessarily willing to embrace what innovation takes to succeed. For example, failing constantly until we unfail. And this is something I want to explore further. Patrik said something interesting about letting the big guys feel like they make the decision, when you actually lay all the pieces out so that the puzzle almost completes itself. But there's a big difference in just presenting the puzzle, and letting them place the final piece and feel good about their oh so genius mind. Feed their ego. I'll remember this.

I'm curious to see how my attitude is going to be towards business after this course, when I'm hopefully more knowledgeable in the area. Because as of now, one part of me frankly dislikes anything business. It doesn't interest me. Parts of it even repulses me. Money-making machines with poor ethics and a broken moral compass is just not something that I vibe with. But I understand that this reaction is a result of bad experiences. It's not true that all business is bad. Magnus said it very well: that even organizations with altruistic incentives need to consider the BMC aspects because that's what's going to drive the ability to do good. That spoke to the other part of me which is also curious about the entrepreneurial spirit, about realizing big things, raising money and doing it for good. People often ask me why I don't start my own business. And that question kind of triggers me. So it will be interesting to see whether I'll get more insight in where I actually stand in this question the upcoming months.

## Future actions
The first I'll focus on is taking steps to becoming more comfortable working with complexity. Instead of getting overwhelmed and lost in it, I need to use my strategic and organizational skills to break it down to smaller pieces that are possible to work with. Until this point, I've converged, and now it might be a good time to diverge. In upcoming projects, mapping out complexity to reveal crucial pain points and opportunities is something I want to practically implement. I'll also remember that constraints is my friend ‚Äì sometimes limiting a scope is more productive than trying to cover everything or trying to perfect.

I'll also work on calibrating my mindset to better match my capabilities, as well as focus more on my capabilities. From working with my Personal Development Plan, I've realized how much I actually belittle myself internally and that the way I think of myself doesn't match what I actually do. So, I'll continue taking actions that are uncomfortable but needed to continuously convince my mind of my capabilities. One example is challenging my discomfort in speaking (which is improving, though) with speaking even more authentically. Because I've seen that this is when people are actually most likely to listen to what I have to say.

Speaking of capabilities and limiting beliefs, I'll continue working on normalizing failure. Not only was that liberating, but I think very strengthening in the long run. If I didn't get so caught up with failure, I believe I would be much more willing to take risk and put myself out there. It's just a mindset. 

Finally, I want to reiterate gut feeling. I don't think we give ourselves and others enough credit for following gut feeling, especially in business. We know that people lie and don't always say what they actually mean. From here on I'll try to be extra attentive to people's reactions as opposed to their words and see how much I can intuitively pick up on that might point to authenticity or misalignment. 

## Methods & tools
BMC is a practical tool I can use to deal with the complexity and interrelational nature of innovation. I believe tools like these are very useful to me since I can easily get caught up in inquisitive loops and existential rabbit holes in my mind. These might be good ways to ground me in something concrete rather than philosophical. 

A common mistake is to treat BMC as a way to check boxes. We saw in the lecture/workshop that it can be a very useful tool to prototype business, for example. When starting to put things out there to experiment with how they affect each other. But I can also see a risk with oversimplifying the complex reality. In the end, it needs to be continuously tested and validated against reality to work. So, it might be useful in early-stage prototyping when visualization is beneficial of fluid ideas. Maybe not as useful when dealing with systemic or societal problems that might not translate so well into business logic.

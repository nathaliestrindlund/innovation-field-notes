# Key learnings
This week was about understanding what a problem really IS – and what is worth solving. We might commonly use the word *problem* in a way that covers a wide range of situations. But in fact, a problem is more specific than we might think. A problem is something formulated in a way that includes:
- **Someone having the problem**: a real type of person in a real situation.
- **Something they're trying to do**: a concrete task or goal.
- **Frictions or pain points:** the specific thing that makes the task harder, slower, more expensive, more stressful or impossible. 
- **Consequences if not addressed**: real and recurring financial, physical or emotional cost. 
Without these things, a problem is a wish. A good problem statement is actionable. If it's not actionable, you're gonna waste a lot of time on uncertainties and lack of clarity. It has to make sense. 

When we think we know something, we should ask ourselves why we think we know that. How do we know that a problem is a problem? A useful signifier is whether people are talking about it. When deciding whether to pursue our work to address our problem, we should ask ourselves "Do people actually care about this?" That’s a very valid question. Is it worth solving if no one cares about it?

Another good thing is to write down all of the assumptions we have. Assumptions aren't necessarily bad to have, but it's really important to acknowledge that they are assumptions – because they will affect how we approach a problem and might point to where we need more information to validate something before moving on. It's also a very good idea to write down explicit things we need to validate with our current problem formulation.

We also need to understand who is suffering from our suggested problem. This is rather about understanding who our stakeholders are, how they are affected by this problem and to what degree. Maybe who we think experiences the problem aren’t the ones experiencing it the most. 

# Personal reflections
I think we benefit from adopting a pretty sharp attitude in this part of the innovation process. This is one of the areas where critical thinking really is important, if not crucial. Here, we should act as a knife. Cut through the illusions, dissect assumptions and make razor sharp definitions. And the more preparation we do to sharpen the knife, the cleaner and easier the cut is gonna be. 

However, it's also important not to narrow down too soon. There needs to be a balance between being specific enough to progress, but at the same time ensuring that we focus on the RIGHT THING. Otherwise everything following in our process will focus on the wrong things. This is a crucial tradeoff. And how do we know when it's time to narrow down? How do we know when we have enough validated to define our problem as "real" and "meaningful"? Maybe we need some... "fingertoppskänsla"? 

One thing I reacted to this week was a strong reaction in the class about having to prepare and conduct interviews in a short time. Even though we've been hinted about this the whole week with lectures on "How do we validate a problem? Talk to people!", and the question "Is this problem accessible?" means we need to ask ourselves whether we can find people to talk to or if we need to rethink. This showed a clear divide in the class between those who adopted a mindset of resisting because of challenging conditions, and those who adapted to the circumstances and acted on what they could. This was a clear example of how mindset affects our capability to act. And as we know, change can't happen without action.

Another thing I noticed was how my interest in my problem changed throughout the week. Initially, I was extremely uninterested in my problem about how elderly struggle with paying their bills electronically. But the deeper we dug into it, the more I learned about the structural barriers causing these issues and how it affects not only the elderly and impaired people but also the people around them. Through the interviews, I heard family members say that they know that they are actually not allowed to help the parent the way they do, but they gladly do it because they know that their parent actually relies on them with their life. And as their parent has taken care of them their whole life, they see this as a way to give back to them. These aspects of strong relationships and honoring your parents and your own history, were heartwarming aspects of the problem I did not expect. This made me care so much more about digital banking systems because they revealed to be related to something truly and deeply human.

# Questions & curiosities
How to handle contradictions. When some people say one thing, and others say the opposite, how should we manage that? Neither is right and neither is wrong, because we're talking about experiences. 

There was also something I found to be especially difficult to interpret in the interviews. And that was the family members avoiding to talk about their own feelings regarding their loved one struggling with basic stuff to function in a society. Instead, they talked about the difficulties that their parent experienced. Maybe because they felt like that's what matters in this situation. But I want the family members to feel cared for, too. Their feelings matter, too. And even though I really tried to nudge the conversation to them and their feelings, it seemed like the interview type of conversation didn't really allow that kind of deep and vulnerable talk.

So, interviews as a format affects what people feel comfortable sharing. Maybe one alternative could be to have several conversations over a longer period of time. That way, we might build up the trust for the interviewees to feel more comfortable sharing their true feelings, and also cover different contexts that can all enable different emotions and experiences. 

# Future actions
In the future, I will be very specific with noting what I *believe* and what I *know* about a problem I'm working with. Just writing down what assumptions that lie underneath our statement showed how much we're actually resting on speculation rather than concrete evidence. 

Secondly, I will practise seeing more opportunities than blockages. Yes, our conditions for succeeding with our interviews were challenging, but we got them in the end. My dad always used to say "Anything is possible – you just have to see it" and I think he had a very valid point. Our mindset really affects our capabilities. 

That's also related to my capability of acting rather than thinking. I'm practising this a lot, of just DOING it and not get stuck in overthinking. And even though this part of the process (validating our problem) requires a lot of thinking, it also requires active decisions about when to switch our focus to another area or gather more information. And I want to get better at just asking people instead of worrying what they'll think. Most often, people actually want to help you if you're working for a good cause. 

# Methods & tools
**Interviews**. They are powerful to understand people's lived experiences and uncovering parts of a problem that are difficult to see in secondary research. Through interviews, we can observe people's reactions to certain words or phrases, hear their tone of voice and observe their body language, all of them revealing nuanced hints about how they actually feel about certain situations. But of course, they are limited in several ways. There can be communication issues leading to misinterpretation of questions, uncomfort in being interviewed, or a poor rapport between the interviewer and the interviewee. The format can make people adapt to whats "socially acceptable" rather than their authentic emotions and thoughts.

Analysing data with **affinity diagram**. This tool helps us structure large amounts of qualitative data and seeing patterns and connections within them. Clustering different insights together can make it easier to identify recurring themes and contradictions within them regarding needs, pain points, wants and experiences. It might be good for identifying problems in a more general sense rather than going into detail for one person. It also proved to be a difficult task to carry out as it requires sharp attention and logic to group insights together in a meaningful way. We need to be careful not to oversimplify experiences or labelling them in ways that don't make sense.

# Resources
**Rob Fitzpatrick – The Mom Test** (book)
This book has been recommended to us several times during this program. It focuses on how to ask better questions when validating ideas and explains how people are often too nice or polite when you ask them about what they think about your idea. Obviously, we want to avoid this distorted feedback, and this book can help us better understand how to do that.

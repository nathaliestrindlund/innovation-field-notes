# 12 – Innovation Field Notes – Value Proposition
## Key learnings
This week has been all about *value logic.* Not just describing a problem, and not going into any solution space yet, but starting to articulate *what* kind of value or benefits we can provide to different stakeholders depending on what we know about the problem so far. What's in it for people? This is different from explaining *how* a solution could provide this value. 

To be able to articulate your value logic, you need to spend time with your problem. This is how you really understand it. This is how you uncover what it's all really about, underneath all the assumptions, generalizations and noise. This is how you see how the pieces fit together and can identify real cause-and-effects chains within the context. Maybe the most important thing about discovering real needs is to twist and turn the problem and talk to as many people as possible. We need to be patient. It can't be rushed.

The same goes for analyzing data. This week, we continued working with Affinity Diagramming, but now to analyze our own interview data from last week. And even though this method provides some structure, the process is messy and difficult. It requires careful handling and not jumping to conclusions too quickly. We need to acknowledge that the analysis also builds on our interpretation of what someone says and does, so there's some level of subjectivity. Data is complex. But eventually, you notice that it kind of starts speaking to you. Insights come as little whispers. And we need to be attentive to what they tell us.

However, we also need to be aware of the fact that, even now, nothing is set in stone. We're still "prototyping" what kind of value is meaningful to provide depending on how our understanding of the problem evolves. One problem can actually be articulated in many, many different ways. And the way we formulate it matters a lot. Not only what words we choose, but in what order they're placed and what emphasis we give them. The smallest tweak can make a big difference in its meaning.

In addition to a Problem Statement (what the problem is) and Value Proposal (what value we can provide for people), we worked with defining a Purpose statement (what we intend to do), Impact Ambition (what change we strive to achieve), and How Might We Statement. Each of these put a different lens on the problem and how we approach it – without proposing any solution or suggestion about how to solve the problem. 

We also engaged with each other's problems by providing critique through one of these roles:
- The Sceptic (pushes for pain level and consequences)
- The Outsider (pushes for clarity of the problem)
- The Stakeholder (pushes for tensions between different stakeholders)
These perspectives were very valuable to both adopt and listen to as they focus on specific dimensions that can reveal strengths and weaknesses in our current problem formulation.

## Personal reflections
One of our interviews captured an extreme user case that revealed many underlying issues at once. Even though extreme cases are rare, I think they can be very valuable to include as they exemplify how multiple different aspects of our problem are interconnected and can practically play out in people's lives. This interview gave us a much clearer understanding of the magnitude of our problem that the interviews with typical users did not. 

I found it very valuable to stay with our problem and be forced to reframe it time and time again. The more we experimented with this, the more I realized how many assumptions were actually woven into the words we chose. It's actually hard to write these statements without relying on any assumption. Following the statement "templates" provided in the workshops really helped seeing this clearer. 

This week, I've also become aware of myself often feeling more engaged with theory than practise. I feel more stimulated when being intellectually challenged and getting opportunities to widen my own perspectives, finding the interesting questions, rather than sharpening practical skills like drawing a specific diagram the "right way".  I've noticed that I feel constrained by the rigidity and annoyed when I'm not free to express my thoughts as they come, when I have to put them into predefined frames. So, the practical tasks we're engaging with during these weeks bring me closer to myself, in a way. I'm learning that maybe I am a change agent that is stronger, or more interested, in using my mind than practical skills. I keep telling myself that I lack practical skills, but maybe I'm not lacking. Maybe I'm rather discovering the strengths I *do* have. One can't have everything. But I also realize that practical frameworks are valuable for structuring our thoughts, of course. It's a tradeoff. And I need to continue exploring what methods work best for me to think deeply and formulate actionable insights while not getting lost in abstraction.

What prevents abstraction is facts, which is represented by data. This week, I've thought a lot about how a previous course emphasized the responsibility we have in collecting and handling data and ensuring that it's both authentic, inclusive, ethical and of course legal. I experienced a big difference in analyzing the synthesized interview data last week and our own data this week. Affinity Diagramming is limited in the way it relies on identifying relationships between isolated snippets of data. But in reality, nothing exists in isolation. When analyzing our own data, there were multiple occasions when someone first identified similarities in two quotes, but when we explained what the interviewee actually meant, it was clear that there really was no correlation between the two quotes. Our background knowledge of the data enabled us to correct these misinterpretations. On the contrary, we could only make assumptions with the synthesized data. So, without being aware of the context in which the data was collected, you risk identifying patterns that don't actually exist. This becomes critical when taking into account that AI models increasingly perform data analysis for us. AI doesn't question the origin, context or ethical implications of the data, it just performs the task. If we don't provide it with structured and well-curated data, the insights it generates can be highly misleading. So, apply AI with great care here.

## Questions & curiosities
At the same time as us needing to spend time with a problem to understand it, the world and the components that make up the context change – faster and faster. This is a stress. We need to spend enough time to dig deep enough, but also move fast. Act rather than just think. This is a difficult tension that I keep coming back to. It makes me feel unsure about how to navigate and trust myself that I have enough evidence to back up my decision about where and how to move forward. 

Another thing that's been on my mind from the presentations in class is the fact that, sometimes, we really want something to be labelled as a problem because we care about it ourselves. But in reality, it might not be that big of an issue. Sometimes, we might have to detach from our own egos and look at things from a bigger perspective to understand whether it's actually worth addressing, or if we just want to do it for personal reasons. I believe that, without even realizing it, we can care a lot about situations or tensions where we've personally experienced shame, embarrassment, exclusion, fear, sadness, loneliness or any other negative feeling – and we want redemption for it. We want to solve that "problem" because we believe then our emotional wound would be healed. So, perhaps it's worth asking ourselves "Am I driven by personal or societal incentives here"?

I also thought it was a bit funny how, no matter what problem we're working with, everyone kind of came to the same conclusion that it's actually a *systemic* problem. Is every problem systemic?

## Future actions
I will be mindful about not rushing to conclusions. I'll stay in the problem and value logic without thinking about solutions, but in a structured way so I'm still grounded in evidence. When I think my statements are complete, I'll continue to refine and reformulate them to actively challenge the assumptions embedded in them. I'll collect a lot of data by talking to many people to validate that people actually care about my problem and whether the value I propose actually resonates with their reality. 

Instead of waiting for everything to feel "clear enough" I will define small and actionable steps that reduce uncertainty and drive the next iteration. It's okay to have assumptions and knowledge gaps, in fact we might always have them, just be sure to be honest about what they are. 

## Methods & tools
**Affinity Diagramming**. This can be good for identifying patterns and themes in big amounts of data. It's good because it's flexible and dynamic. You kind of continuously mold the diagram and it evolves multiple times throughout the activity. It's a quick and hands-on way to organize and categorize data. But as I wrote above, it's limited by the fact that it takes things out of context, which can be misleading if not carefully handled.

**Iterative framing** and **validation tools**. Actively rewriting and testing different versions of our Problem Statement, Value Proposal, Purpose Statement, Impact Ambition, and How might We statement really challenges the assumptions behind them. Combining this iterative reframing with validation tools like interviews provides clarity and increased accuracy, and keeps us moving despite uncertainty. Agility makes us adaptable even when circumstances are changing. However, it comes to a point when we need to come to definite formulations as it can be confusing to constantly change them. Especially when working with partners or clients who might not be involved in every part of your process who still need to be assured about what we're doing and where we're going.

# W14 – Innovation Field Notes – Conceptualization
## Key learnings
Very few things are 100% a product or a 100% a service. They are somewhere in between. Any successful solution requires a successful organization running behind it. The employees matter as much as the users – because they are both part of an inter-relational system that enables them both to thrive. This is where Service Design comes into place. It considers the whole ecosystem by directly improving the employee's experience, and indirectly the end user's. 

This also suggests that value isn't created in a single touchpoint but rather over multiple, and over a period of time. Just like we learned a few weeks ago about a real problem being an experienced friction over time, real value might emerge in interconnected beneficial experiences over time. This is heavily connected to system's thinking.

We also saw that validation is heavily interwoven into the conceptualization phase. This is when ideas are transformed into something more grounded, something closer to a feasible solution. To inform us about this, we can use tools like Customer Journey mapping and Service Blueprints, which give us an overview on both human experiences and the different parts needed to realize our concept. Through this, we can identify crucial touchpoints and pain points that require further exploration through for example a prototyping. 

## Personal reflections
I'm starting to feel a general transition in the program, from imagining *what could be* to *what could actually work in reality*. I appreciate this feasibility focus because this is what I've been lacking before. We're challenged to investigate and validate our concepts in a way that constantly makes it possible for them to be proven wrong. And this is the only real way forward. Then it's not just a guess anymore, but a continuously improved educated guess. 

We're also prompted to think about who would pay for our idea. Who would provide it? How would they be aware of its existence? These questions are crucial for any real-world scenario. But it's also difficult. The only way to understand this better is talking to people. Talking to stakeholders. This is also when things starts getting difficult for real.

Now we are faced with tough questions and are being forced to challenge our idea with hard facts and truths that might compromise its potential. As we interviewed different stakeholders throughout the week, piece by piece of our initial ideas had to be scrapped either because we created them based on fractured information or pure assumptions that turned out to be wrong – or had actually already been solved without us knowing it. This shows the need for us to be *resilient* as change agents, because to find something that not only shows promise but also works in reality, you're likely going to have to rethink, rethink, and rethink again, and we need to be persistent during that process even though it can sometimes feel a bit hopeless. What's important to remember is that the more we discover, the closer we get to the *real needs* – which is our ultimate goal. 

## Questions & curiosities
In the material studied, there were lots of emphasis on designing for the employee. However, there was little focus on this during the following workshops and discussions. So even though I'm aware of this being important – I'm still a bit lost on how to accomplish this and balance a design to not only provide value for end users but also employees. I think this also requires a lot of ground work as organizational goals, hierarchies, power dynamics and cultures can affect employees in ways that might be difficult to understand without studying it over a longer period of time. And it might be even harder to design solutions that effectively meet all those dimensions. How much can, and should, we actually affect?

This week, I also attended an [external lecture on Planterary Boundaries](https://extlect.mau.se/?query=69c5a67125a4206a9b2c4b4f) to increase my knowledge in how to design sustainable innovation. I feel like sustainability and ethics are two huge topics that we constantly mention but don't work deeply enough with. These are two of the topics I'm mostly concerned with myself but don't feel like we really cover a lot in class or have a clear enough grasp of how to approach. It doesn't matter that we know that our concepts *should* be ethical and sustainable if we lack the knowledge of how to actually realize that. I wish that we would have more emphasis on these areas, especially when this course is about developing our capabilities to create responsible innovation.

## Future actions
Next time I want to more deliberately map out both the end user and employee perspectives of the system. Now, we had more focus on the end user side and are still not clear about how our concept would work from the inside. This also relates to understanding who our customer is. Find out who wants our concept – and involve them in the process early to understand their perspective and adapt the concept to fit them as well. I think this can also help me become more aware of where the actual breaking points exist that prevent a solution from being successful, as we learned that it's usually a result from a misalignment between the end user needs and organizational operation.

As this week also showed how it's easy to get attached to our ideas and resisting letting them go, this is something I can get more comfortable with. I usually say "a good solution is a *precise* solution", but I might still be less willing of giving up on my original ideas than I want to admit.

I think what could help me in this is to adopt faster iterations of validation. Instead of refining, test and confirm, or pivot. As we know, it's cheaper now than later. The fastest way to a precise solution is most likely the one that continuously calibrates rather than taking long shots in the wrong direction – because we've seen that it's really unlikely to anticipate what direction we're actually going to end up in. 

## Methods & tools
**Customer Journey Mapping**: this is a tool to map out human experiences tied to specific, or all, parts of a concept. These experiences can be either end users, stakeholders or employees and can be isolated flows or end-to-end experiences. Customer Journeys help us understand experiences and identify pain points or opportunities for creating value. 

**Service Blueprint**: in contrast to a Customer Journey map, a Service Blueprint maps out the things involved in the service provider's organization (the people, props and processes). A service blueprint is more comprehensive as it shows both what's frontstage and backstage of a service. 

Both of these tools seem useful for filling in the blanks when going from idea to implementation. They show how experiences unfold over time and across different actors. But again, if they're only grounded on assumptions, they can reinforce an illusion that points us in a direction that doesn't actually provide real value or meet real needs. Therefore, they should be continuously updated to ensure that we're building off of real data and might be better viewed as prototyping and communication tools than roadmaps.

## Resources
**Planetary Boundaries – Per Jönsson** (external lecture): this lecture highlighted how complex systems can be observed, monitored quantified to inform us about existing changes. It emphasized why assumptions need to be validated: because when we have quantified data, we can use that to make people believe us. This was inspirational to me as I clearly see why we need to involve stakeholders early into our innovation process – because collecting that real data gives us credibility. It can be the determining factor when arguing for the need of a change initiative.

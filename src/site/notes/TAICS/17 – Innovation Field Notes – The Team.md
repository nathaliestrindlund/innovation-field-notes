# W17 – Innovation Field Notes – The Team
## Key learnings
Most of our sources come to the same conclusion. No matter how much we try to rationalize and structure our innovation work with frameworks and models – we simply can't escape unpredictability of the innovation ecosystem and can never be sure to achieve any specific, defined outcome. It's pretty ironic. 

Timing is one very apparent example of this. No matter how well we think we're prepared for a challenge, how much we've read up and validated about it, how great of a solution we built – it can simply fail because the time wasn't right for that particular thing to come into reality. We need to match these "windows of opportunity" – where there's alignment between our own idea, the place, available resources and the right *time*. We're not joking when labelling the innovation ecosystem as "complex". Because apparently, in the end it almost comes down to not-yet-existing, unforeseeable, spiritual factors. 

When it comes to the team, we know that it's one of the most important things that investors etc look at when deciding whether to invest in us or not. So how do we make them believe in us? We talked about many things, like showing that we've made our research and presenting validated data in numbers, being transparent about what we know as well as we don't know, build your reputation and work with "good" names in your advisory board or as partners. We can also partner with researchers (especially those with credible names) which can help us collect a good research foundation to build from. 

## Personal reflections
Timing is an interesting thing. In the previous course, when reading up on certain characteristics of innovators and entrepreneurs, listening to hunches or your intuition was often mentioned, which I found interesting then and still do. The same thing is often highlighted by my beloved Rick Rubin. And our discussions in the seminar this week came to the same topic. There's this notion that we don't own our ideas, we just mediate them (also described by Rick Rubin). Have you noticed sometimes that if you've gotten a really good idea but not acting on it – and then you see this idea being realized by someone else? Ultimately, perhaps intuition is still our strongest tool to navigate the complex and uncertain world around us. 

In terms of making others believe us, I think it's kind of the same thing here as with the unpredictable contextual factors we can't control. We can do everything right to make people believe us, but still, not everyone comes with the same credibility. Some things are out of our control, like prejudice. People do assume things about us no matter what we do, based on their own history, experience and assumptions. That's simply out of our control, and perhaps the only thing we can do about that is be aware of it and accept it.

## Questions & curiosities
This is a difficult field. Because we're dealing with ill-defined and difficult problems that don't always have an answer. Or we might not be able to trust the answers that we get. Or the answers might be true, but only at this particular moment and for some people, but it's not guaranteed to also be true next week or by other people. Still, we have all of these frameworks, tools and models that provide "ways to deal with them" – but ultimately everything is too complex to comprehend and also unpredictable. 

Since in the end, it's unpredictable and intangible anyway, how useful are all these models and tools we're learning about, really? I think they're good as long as they're viewed as support to us – but we need to know their limitations, and when we're actually working with assumptions, which can generate questionable results, no matter how robust the tools are. But I still find this difficult, to wrap my head around how to be confident in navigating uncertainty.  

## Future actions
I will work on prioritizing what I believe is most relevant to present for people in order to build credibility. I'm going to think about what audience I have, and what they're interested in seeing or being convinced of to build trust in me. 

I also want to become better at knowing and articulating my own strengths. I'm really good at stating my shortcomings. But what *can* I actually bring to the table? I think I would benefit from looking at myself more objectively sometimes and just stating facts rather than expressing how I feel. As a very emotion-oriented person, that's often what I do. So I should continue working with tools such as the Competency chart.

## Methods & tools
**Competency chart:** This tool can be helpful for us to understand our own strengths and shortcomings and what type of role we are best suited to play in a team. It's good for being able to more clearly articulate our value to other people so that they also understand how we can be an asset to them. But obviously, it's very biased on our own perception of ourselves. If we have a skewed self image or low self esteem/self worth, this chart might not at all correspond to our actual capabilities.

**Team chart:** this is a good tool to map out the competences we collectively have in our team and how they could work together. It can help guide us in assigning roles and responsibilities within the team, as well as where we are lacking and need to look for external resources or expert help. However, it lacks many dimensions that makes a good team. It says nothing about social dynamics or communication styles, which are examples of things that can make or break good teamwork.
